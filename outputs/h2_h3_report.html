Community composition vs. aridity

Do arid and humid sites host different microbial communities, and which environmental variable explains that difference best? Bray-Curtis PCoA + PERMANOVA, following Neilson et al. (2017), mSystems.

n = 54 samples in the ordination Distance: Bray-Curtis, OTU-level counts Script: solution.py

The questions

H2 predicts that the most arid sites (Yungay) separate from the least arid sites (Baquedano) along the first PCoA axis of community composition. H3 predicts that soil humidity explains more compositional variance than temperature or elevation (expected R² 0.20–0.50).

Method, in brief

Bray-Curtis dissimilarity was computed between all 54 sequenced samples directly from OTU counts. A classical PCoA (Gower double-centering + eigendecomposition) produced sample coordinates and the % variance explained per axis. To test each environmental variable's association with composition, a univariate PERMANOVA (McArdle & Anderson, 1999 permutations) was run separately for humidity, temperature, and elevation — each on its own maximum available sample subset, since not every sample has every variable recorded.

Combined, the three variables explain 2.5–2.6× more variance than any single one (R² = 0.129 vs. ~0.05), still short of the 0.20–0.50 range anticipated by H3 but a much better picture of what's going on. The sum of the three individual R² values (0.158) is only slightly above the combined model (0.129) — a redundancy of just 0.029, meaning the variables are mostly complementary rather than measuring the same thing twice. The weak univariate results, in other words, were partly an artifact of testing one variable at a time rather than evidence that the environment doesn't matter.

Is elevation a proxy for humidity and temperature?

Elevation was the single best individual predictor of composition in the univariate PERMANOVA. Two physical mechanisms could explain that: orographic precipitation (higher elevation → more rain → wetter soil) and the standard atmospheric lapse rate (higher elevation → colder). If elevation is really just a stand-in for humidity and temperature, that would explain why it performs about as well as either of them alone.

Yes — elevation is strongly and significantly related to both (p < 10⁻²⁰ for each). Higher sites are wetter (consistent with orographic precipitation on the Andean slopes) and colder (consistent with the standard lapse rate). Elevation is, physically, a joint proxy for both drivers at once.

Compared on the exact same 51 samples, elevation edges out humidity as an explanatory variable for composition — but only barely (0.0502 vs. 0.0498, essentially a tie). That's a sensible result given how strongly elevation tracks both humidity and temperature: it isn't a fundamentally better variable, it's a compressed summary of two variables that individually explain a similar, modest share of composition. Practically, this means elevation could stand in for either measurement where field logistics make humidity or temperature sensors hard to deploy — but it doesn't unlock materially more explanatory power than measuring humidity directly.

Biological interpretation

H2 partially holds: composition does shift along the aridity gradient, but the axes explain a small share of total variance (8.3% + 5.7%) — expected for sparse, high-richness OTU tables, where most variation is spread across many minor axes rather than concentrated in the first two. H3 does not hold as stated at the univariate level: humidity explains the least variance of the three variables tested alone (R² = 0.050). But combined, the three variables reach R² = 0.129 — still below the 0.20–0.50 range anticipated, yet 2.5× higher than any single variable, with little redundancy between them. The likely explanation isn't that the environment doesn't matter, but that (a) we used Bray-Curtis on raw OTUs rather than the paper's phylogeny-aware UniFrac, which is noisier for this kind of sparse, rare-taxon-heavy data, and (b) desert soil composition here responds to humidity, temperature, and elevation jointly rather than to any one acting alone.

Open questions

  • A phylogeny-aware distance (weighted/unweighted UniFrac, as in the original paper) would need a representative OTU tree, which this course dataset doesn't include — worth testing if that changes the R² picture.
  • The three hollow grey points (BAQ4697) sit apart from the rest along PC1/PC2 — worth checking whether losing their environmental data also reflects something unusual about their community.
